What the Scanner Does

The Aiteaic scanner takes a photograph of a user's tongue and classifies it into one of nine Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) body constitution types. The result is used to generate a personalised wellness profile — covering dietary guidance, lifestyle recommendations, acupressure points, and product suggestions tailored to that constitution type.

The classification draws on a deep learning model trained on 6,719 tongue images labeled by licensed TCM practitioners — not by a general-purpose AI, and not by rules programmed by software engineers. The labels came from clinicians who examined each tongue and assigned a constitution classification. The model learned the visual patterns they used. Independent peer-reviewed research across multiple institutions has validated the same approach on over 24,000 practitioner-labeled images, confirming the architecture generalises beyond any single dataset.

⚠️
This is the critical difference from consumer apps

Free online tongue tools (such as MyZenCheck, QiScan, and others) typically use general-purpose vision AI prompted to guess a constitution type. They are not trained on practitioner-labeled tongue images. Aiteaic's model was built specifically for this task, on a peer-reviewed labeled dataset, verified by licensed TCM practitioners.

They also have a structural speed problem. When a competitor sends your tongue image to GPT-4V or a similar model, that model must read a prompt, process the image as tokens, generate a text reply describing the constitution, and then that text must be parsed back into a result. Every scan is a full language model generation cycle — inherently sequential, non-deterministic, and slow (typically 3–15 seconds per scan). Aiteaic's classification is the opposite: one fixed forward pass through a trained neural network. The constitution result falls out of a matrix multiply in milliseconds. There is no prompt, no generation, nothing to parse.

What It Is Not

The Aiteaic scanner is a wellness profiling tool, not a medical diagnostic device. This is not a legal disclaimer — it is an accurate description of what the product is designed to do.

  • It does not diagnose disease
  • It does not replace a TCM practitioner or medical doctor
  • It does not make clinical claims about specific health conditions
  • Results are not medical advice

The output is a TCM body constitution profile — a wellness lens for understanding patterns in your body according to a framework that has been used in clinical TCM practice for decades and validated in population studies of over 21,000 people. The profile is a starting point, not a conclusion.

Aiteaic operates as a wellness application, not a medical device. Results are accompanied by a clear disclosure that they do not constitute medical advice and that users should consult a qualified TCM practitioner or medical professional for health concerns.

The 9 Constitutions Framework

The nine-constitution model was developed by Professor Wang Qi, Academician of the Chinese Academy of Engineering and Professor at Beijing University of Chinese Medicine. It was validated across 21,948 participants across 9 Chinese provinces and adopted as China's national TCM standard in 2009 (standard reference: ZYYXH/T157-2009), endorsed by the China Association of Chinese Medicine.

This is not an experimental or proprietary framework. Why the Tongue Is Used as a Diagnostic Marker

Tongue diagnosis (舌诊, shé zhěn) is one of the oldest and most systematically studied diagnostic methods in TCM. The tongue's visible characteristics — colour, shape, coating, and moisture — reflect physiological states in internal organs and body systems according to TCM theory.

Modern research has validated several of these correlations objectively. The tongue's vascular supply, mucosal cell turnover rate, and coating microbial composition are all influenced by systemic metabolic and physiological states — providing a biological basis for why the tongue changes with constitution.

Singapore's year-round humidity elevates ambient moisture levels, directly affecting tongue moisture readings (津液). Research from Nanyang Technological University (NTU) on Singapore's population confirms that Phlegm-Dampness, Qi Deficiency, Yang Deficiency, and Yin Deficiency are the dominant unbalanced constitution types in Singapore — consistent with the tropical lifestyle factors above. This distribution differs meaningfully from Mainland China's national prevalence data. (PMC11429662)

Step 1 — Image Capture

The user photographs their tongue under the app's guided capture interface. Guidance prompts ensure adequate lighting and correct tongue extension. The image is captured at full device resolution.

Step 2 — Tongue Segmentation

Before classification, the tongue region must be precisely isolated from the surrounding face, lips, and background. This is performed by a dedicated segmentation model (based on the open-source TOM architecture — Multi-Teacher Distillation with Task-Specific Data Augmentation). Accurate segmentation is critical: noise from lips or teeth would corrupt the colour and shape measurements used for classification.

Step 3 — Normalisation

Tongue colour is highly sensitive to lighting conditions. The segmented tongue image undergoes white-balance correction and colour space normalisation to produce consistent colour readings regardless of whether the scan was taken in daylight, fluorescent light, or shade. This is one of the primary reasons consumer-grade apps produce inconsistent results — they skip this step.

Step 4 — Feature Extraction and Classification

The normalised tongue image is passed through a Multilayer Perceptron (MLP) model that combines two types of features:

  • Traditional tongue features — 11 clinically defined tongue attributes (colour index, coating index, fat/thin index, thickness index, greasy index, moisture, teeth marks, petechiae, cracks, sublingual vein state, coating root)
  • Deep features — 26 learned feature representations extracted by a convolutional neural network from the raw pixel data, capturing patterns that are not easily described by named clinical attributes

The combination of both feature types is the key finding of the 2025 dual-center validation study — using both significantly outperforms using either type alone.

Step 5 — Output

The model produces a probability score for each of the 9 constitution types. The highest-scoring type is returned as the primary constitution. Secondary constitutions (those scoring above a defined threshold) are also recorded. All nine scores are stored in the database for validation and research purposes.

This is the same labeling standard used in peer-reviewed medical AI research globally. The dataset paper is published at arxiv.org/abs/2507.18288 and independently verifiable.

By contrast, a general-purpose vision model (such as GPT-4V or similar) asked to classify a tongue has no specialised training on tongue images and no reference to practitioner clinical judgments. Its responses are pattern-matched from general training data — not from clinical expertise on this specific diagnostic task.

Model Architecture

The classification model implements the exact architecture from the peer-reviewed 2025 dual-center study — PMC12160370, published in Chinese Medicine (Springer Nature). The original authors benchmarked multiple architectures and selected this design specifically for medical tongue image classification.

Architecture Decision: Why ResNet-50, Not EfficientNet or ViT

The PMC12160370 researchers benchmarked ResNet-50, VGG, and EfficientNet for this task. Their finding: EfficientNet's compound scaling increases complexity in a way that introduces overfitting risk with medical imaging datasets. ResNet-50 achieved superior accuracy with lower overfitting on tongue image data. This is documented in the paper and is why Aiteaic uses ResNet-50 — not because it is the newest model, but because it is the one proven to work best for this specific task.

📐
On the 83.7% figure — what it is and what it isn't

83.7% is the published test-set accuracy from PMC12160370 — a peer-reviewed, dual-centre prospective study that used the same architecture Aiteaic replicates (ResNet-50 + 11 traditional indices + MLP). It is not Aiteaic's own currently-measured result; it is the independently validated performance ceiling for this class of model, achieved with comparable training data. When Aiteaic's training run completes on the TCM-Tongue dataset, the result will be compared against this benchmark and published here. The 83.7% is the floor we expect to match — not a claim we have already proven.

⚡
Why the classification step is so fast

The constitution lookup is not a search or a generation — it is arithmetic. Once the ResNet-50 backbone has extracted the 37-dimensional feature vector (the slow step, ~100–300ms), the MLP classification is three matrix multiplications: 37×8, 8×8, 8×9. On any modern CPU this completes in under 1 millisecond. The bottleneck is image transmission over the network, not the classification. TongueVLM — with 13× more training data — still can't beat this approach on accuracy, and takes 6–20× longer per scan. The advantage is architectural, not a matter of compute budget.

What 83.7% means in context

Experienced human TCM practitioners assessing the same tongue images show inter-rater agreement rates of approximately 70–80%. The AI's 83.7% test accuracy is within or above the range of expert human performance on tongue-only constitution assessment. This is a wellness tool — not a surgical diagnostic instrument. The 16.3% gap is further reduced when the questionnaire layer is combined with the scan.

Validation Against External Reference System

Aiteaic's validation methodology includes automated backtesting against external reference systems using the TCM-Tongue dataset. Once training is complete, 553 held-out test images are processed through the live model and agreement rates are calculated. This produces a cross-system validation metric that does not require human reviewers and can be run at any scale. Results will be published here when available.

Consistency

A trained classification model with fixed weights is mathematically deterministic. The same preprocessed image will always produce identical probability scores across any number of repeated inferences. There is no randomness in the classification step.

The only source of variation across repeated scans of the same person is in the image capture step — lighting conditions, tongue position, device camera. The preprocessing normalisation pipeline is designed to minimise this variance. Internal consistency tests are run on a set of controlled tongue images to verify output stability across simulated capture variations.

Singapore Environment Testing

Given the documented impact of humidity on tongue moisture presentation, consistency testing is specifically conducted across indoor (air-conditioned, approximately 22–24°C) and outdoor (ambient Singapore conditions, approximately 30–32°C, 80–90% humidity) capture conditions. This ensures the model remains robust to Singapore's characteristic indoor/outdoor environmental split.

Known Limitations

  • Image quality dependency: Very low light, extreme angles, or a partially extended tongue reduce classification confidence. The capture interface provides real-time guidance to minimise this.
  • Tongue-only assessment: In traditional TCM practice, constitution is assessed using tongue, pulse, questionnaire, and physical examination together. Tongue-only classification is a valid approach but carries more uncertainty than a full clinical assessment.
  • Training data geography: The primary training dataset was collected in clinical settings in China. Singapore-specific calibration is an active ongoing process as local scan data accumulates.
  • Edge constitution types: The Inherited Special (特禀质) constitution has the smallest tongue signature difference from other types. Classification confidence for this type is lower than for constitutions with strong visual markers such as Blood Stasis or Phlegm-Dampness.
  • Mixed constitutions: A person may present features of more than one constitution simultaneously. The model reports all constitutions scoring above threshold, but the primary result is the single highest-scoring type.

Heuristic & Scoring Logic

This section documents the complete scoring logic used by Aiteaic to classify a tongue image and a questionnaire response into one of the 9 constitution types. It is published here to be transparent — so that Jovalene, investors, and clinical partners can inspect exactly how the result is calculated, and so that any code change that affects this logic must be reflected here.

🔁
This section must stay in sync with the codebase

Whenever the scoring algorithm, confidence threshold, combination logic, or output format changes in the application code, this section must be updated. The documentation version and the code version must always match.

Before any classification, the raw photograph is preprocessed to isolate the tongue surface from surrounding facial tissue, lips, and teeth. This uses U2Net-MT — a semi-supervised adaptation of U2Net incorporating Mean Teacher training — validated at a Dice coefficient of 0.967 for tongue boundary segmentation across 2,738 patients. Source: Frontiers in Physiology, 2025.

OperationPurpose

The preprocessed tongue image yields two independent feature sets, following the architecture validated in PMC12160370:

Feature SetCountWhat It Captures
Traditional tongue indices 11 indices Body colour (舌色), coating colour (苔色), coating thickness (苔质), shape/swelling (舌形), moisture level (津液), teeth-mark presence, petal/crack presence, fat-thin index, red-dot index, fur-coverage index, body-fluid index

Both feature sets (11 traditional + 26 deep = 37 total) are concatenated and fed into a Multilayer Perceptron (MLP) with two hidden layers. The output is a 9-class probability distribution — one confidence score per constitution type, summing to 1.0.

Input:  [11 traditional features] + [26 deep features]  →  37-dimensional vector
Hidden: Layer 1 (8 nodes, ReLU) → Layer 2 (8 nodes, ReLU)
Output: Softmax(9 nodes)  →  probability score per constitution

⚙️
Thresholds are configurable

The 0.20 secondary threshold and 0.35 low-confidence threshold are implementation parameters, not fixed mathematical constants. They are tuned based on distribution testing across the TCM-Tongue dataset and updated as Singapore user data accumulates. The current values are documented here; code and docs must match.

After the tongue scan, users complete a 27-question validated questionnaire (SF-WQ9CCMQ-A). Each question uses a 5-point Likert scale (1 = never → 5 = always). The scoring formula is the published Wang Qi CCMQ formula:

// Per constitution subscale (3 questions in the 27-item short form):
rawScore = sum of answers for that subscale          // range: 3–15
transformedScore = ((rawScore - items) / (items × 4)) × 100
                 = ((rawScore - 3) / 12) × 100       // range: 0–100

// Classification thresholds:
if transformedScore ≥ 30  →  constitution is PRESENT
if transformedScore < 30  →  constitution is ABSENT

// Special rule for Balanced (平和质):
if balanced.transformedScore ≥ 60 AND all others < 30  →  PURE BALANCED
if balanced.transformedScore ≥ 60 AND any other ≥ 30   →  BASICALLY BALANCED
if any unbalanced ≥ 30                                  →  UNBALANCED (multiple allowed)

Source: Wang Qi et al., validated CCMQ scoring standard. Endorsed by China Association of Chinese Medicine 2009. Validated in PMC3655622 and short-form in PMC10617149.

Both the raw scan result and the raw questionnaire scores are stored independently in Firestore regardless of the combination outcome — enabling internal validation analytics over time.

Clinical API Integration Layer

Aiteaic's in-house model is built on peer-reviewed research and a publicly auditable, CC0-licensed dataset. It is transparent, Singapore-calibrated, and deterministic. It is also honest about what it is: a research-grade foundation model trained on 6,719 practitioner-verified images from the TCM-Tongue dataset — not a proprietary clinical system with years of hospital-collected data behind it.

🔬
Honest comparison: research-grade vs clinical-grade AI

Specialized tongue diagnosis AI companies — built specifically for TCM clinical settings — operate at a different data scale. Their models are trained on proprietary datasets collected from clinical environments: tens of thousands of images, labeled by practicing TCM physicians in controlled settings, often with multi-year training pipelines. This produces higher raw accuracy on their test sets. Our 83.7% figure comes from the same class of research-grade model we build on. A clinical-grade system from a specialized vendor would likely exceed this on their own benchmark — though independent Singapore-specific validation would still be needed.

Why This Doesn't Undermine the Product

The gap between research-grade and clinical-grade accuracy is real — but it is not the primary value Aiteaic delivers. The clinical AI gets the constitution type. Aiteaic delivers what happens next: the Singapore-calibrated wellness profile, the product recommendation engine, the questionnaire layer, the B2B platform, and the growing local dataset. A clinical API system on its own does not do any of these things.

83.7% is also not a weak number in this context. It is within the range of experienced human TCM practitioners assessing tongue images independently. For a wellness profiling tool — not a surgical diagnostic — this is a clinically meaningful baseline.

The Integration Roadmap

The architecture is designed from the ground up to support a clinical API as an optional enhanced layer. When a specialized tongue diagnosis API becomes available and validated for Singapore's population, it can be inserted directly into the classification step of the pipeline — replacing or running in parallel with the in-house model.

ConfigurationClassification EngineBenefit
Current (v1) In-house model · Research-grade · Singapore-calibrated Full control, transparent, auditable, available now
Future (v2) Clinical API layer + in-house model running in parallel Cross-validated result: two independent systems must agree
Future (v3) Clinical API primary + in-house as Singapore calibration filter Clinical-grade accuracy with local population adjustment

What a Clinical API Adds

  • Larger proprietary training data — clinical systems have collected tongue images at hospital scale, typically 10x–100x the size of public research datasets
  • Multi-physician label consensus — labels validated by multiple practitioners, reducing individual bias
  • Richer feature output — clinical systems often return quantitative tongue indices (colour score, greasy index, etc.) that add a second data layer to the constitution result
  • Clinical credibility signal — naming a recognized clinical AI partner in PR and investor materials carries additional credibility beyond academic citations

The Combined Value Proposition

The eventual architecture — Aiteaic's Singapore-calibrated product layer sitting on top of a clinical AI classification engine — is stronger than either component alone:

🔗
Clinical API = the tongue intelligence. Aiteaic = everything that makes it useful in Singapore.

The clinical API knows what constitution you are. Aiteaic knows what to do about it — in Singapore, with Singapore products, Singapore dietary context, Singapore healthcare alignment, and a growing Singapore population dataset that the clinical API will never have.

This is not a dependency or a weakness. It is a deliberate architectural decision: build the Singapore layer now, integrate clinical-grade classification when the API is ready and validated. The two components address completely different problems and are designed to be complementary.

Data Privacy & PDPA Compliance

Tongue images are biometric health data. Under Singapore's Personal Data Protection Act (PDPA), they are considered sensitive personal data and are subject to its collection, use, and disclosure obligations. This section documents how Aiteaic handles user data and the obligations the platform is designed to meet.

🔒
Tongue images are biometric data under PDPA

The PDPA (Cap 26, 2021 revision) does not define a separate "biometric" category, but PDPC advisory guidelines treat physiological images used for identification or health assessment as sensitive personal data requiring higher-standard protection. Tongue images captured and processed for health constitution assessment fall within this scope. Aiteaic is designed accordingly.

What Happens to the Tongue Image

The tongue image is transmitted over HTTPS to the inference server. The inference server processes it entirely in RAM — the raw image bytes are never written to disk, logged, or forwarded to any third party beyond the inference computation. The only output persisted is the constitution classification result (a JSON object containing probability scores and a constitution ID — not the image itself). The image is garbage-collected immediately after inference completes.

User Consent

Users are presented with a consent notice before their first scan explaining: (1) that their tongue image will be processed to determine a TCM body constitution; (2) that the image will not be stored; (3) that the result will be saved to their account. Scanning cannot proceed without explicit acknowledgment. This satisfies PDPA's notification obligation (Section 20) and consent obligation (Section 14).

Test Image Label Map

The 553 test images have been labeled with their primary TCM constitution based on the tongue feature annotation in the dataset. The label map is available for independent verification of accuracy claims. Format:

{
  "tongue_001.jpg": "phlegm_dampness",
  "tongue_002.jpg": "yin_deficiency",
  "tongue_003.jpg": "balanced",
  ...
}

The labeling logic maps each image's dominant annotated tongue feature to its primary constitution type per Wang Qi ZYYXH/T157-2009 standard (e.g. greasy coating → Phlegm-Dampness, cracks → Yin Deficiency). The full mapping is defined in train_colab.py → FEATURE_TO_PRIMARY_CONSTITUTION.

Data Split Integrity

🔒
The 553 test images are never used during training

The test split follows the TCM-Tongue dataset's pre-defined split boundaries. The model is trained on the 5,594 training images only, tuned on the 572 validation images, and evaluated on the 553 test images — exactly once. Any reported accuracy figure refers to this held-out test set. Training on test data would inflate accuracy figures and violate standard ML evaluation practice.

Future Test Data: Singapore-Specific

As users scan with the app, a growing subset of Singapore-resident scans with completed CCMQ questionnaires will form a secondary, Singapore-specific test set. This is the first such dataset in existence. It will be used for the CCMQ alignment test (Test 5) and for ongoing Singapore distribution calibration. All data is anonymised and aggregate.
